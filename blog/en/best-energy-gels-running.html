Energy gels have become an essential part of every distance runner's toolkit. Whether you are training for a 10K, a half marathon, or a full marathon, having the right fuel can mean the difference between a personal best and hitting the wall at kilometer 30 (World Athletics) (ACSM).

How energy gels work

Three key factors define a great energy gel for runners:

  • Carbohydrate source: The primary fuel in any gel. Most use maltodextrin, fructose, or a blend of both. The ratio matters: a 2:1 maltodextrin-to-fructose ratio allows your body to absorb up to 90g of carbs per hour, compared to 60g from a single source.
  • Consistency and osmolality: Standard gels are thick and concentrated, requiring water to digest. Isotonic gels are pre-diluted to match your blood's concentration, making them easier on the stomach but larger to carry.
  • Extras: Caffeine, electrolytes (sodium, potassium), BCAAs, and vitamins. Caffeine-enhanced gels can provide a mental and physical boost, especially in the later stages of a race. Electrolytes help prevent cramping.
Tip: Never try a new gel on race day. Always test your nutrition strategy during long training runs. Your stomach needs to adapt to processing fuel while running at race pace. Start experimenting at least 4-6 weeks before your target race.

Frequently asked questions

When should I take an energy gel during a race?

For a marathon, most experts recommend taking your first gel around 45 minutes into the race, then every 30-45 minutes after that. For a half marathon, one gel at 45 minutes and another around 75 minutes is usually sufficient. Always practice your gel strategy during training to find what works best for your stomach.

Do I need to drink water with energy gels?

With most gels, yes. Standard gels are highly concentrated and need water to be absorbed properly. Taking them without water can cause stomach distress and slow absorption. The exception is isotonic gels like SiS GO Isotonic or Enervit Isotonic, which are pre-mixed with water and can be taken without additional fluids. Always check the label of your specific gel.

Can energy gels cause stomach problems?

Yes, stomach distress is the most common complaint with energy gels. This usually happens when you take too many at once, do not drink enough water with non-isotonic gels, or try a new gel on race day without testing it first. To minimize issues, always test gels during training runs, take them with water (unless isotonic), and space them at least 30 minutes apart. Maurten's hydrogel technology was specifically developed to reduce GI issues.

What is the difference between isotonic gels and regular gels?

Regular gels are concentrated carbohydrate solutions that require water to be digested properly. Isotonic gels (like SiS GO and Enervit) already contain the right amount of water to match your body fluid concentration, so they can be consumed without additional water. Isotonic gels are easier on the stomach but are larger and heavier to carry. For races with frequent aid stations, regular gels work fine. For races in remote areas or ultra-distance events, isotonic gels simplify logistics.

How many energy gels should I carry for a marathon?

Plan for 5-7 gels for a full marathon, depending on your pace and how frequently you take them. If you take one every 30 minutes starting at 45 minutes, you will need about 6-7 gels. Many runners carry 4-5 and pick up 1-2 at aid stations. Always carry one extra as a backup. If you use high-carb gels like the Crown HyperGel 45, you may need only 3-4 gels for the same carbohydrate intake.
