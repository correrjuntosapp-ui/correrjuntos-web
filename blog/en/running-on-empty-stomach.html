Running on an Empty Stomach: Benefits, Risks & How to Do It Right

Does it burn more fat? Is it dangerous? Everything science says, myth-free.

Training · Mar 8, 2026 · By Carlos Ruiz · 12 min read

Few practices in the running world generate as much debate as fasted running. For some it is the key to burning fat and improving metabolic performance. For others, a dangerous shortcut to muscle catabolism and hypoglycemia. The reality, as almost always, lies somewhere in the middle.

In this article we will set the myths aside and analyze what scientific evidence really says about running without having eaten breakfast: what happens inside your body, which benefits are real, what risks exist and, above all, how to do it right if you decide to try it.

What happens in your body when you run fasted?

After 8-12 hours without eating (the typical overnight fast), your body undergoes important metabolic changes that directly affect how it obtains energy during exercise:

  • Low liver glycogen: liver glycogen stores are reduced by 50% to 80% during the night. Muscle glycogen, however, remains practically intact because it is only consumed through direct muscle activity.
  • Low insulin, high glucagon: with insulin at minimal levels, your body has the hormonal signal to mobilize stored fats as an energy source. Glucagon and catecholamines (adrenaline) facilitate lipolysis.
  • Greater fatty acid oxidation: when running with little available glucose, your body turns to free fatty acids as fuel earlier and in greater proportion. This is the central mechanism that makes fasted training interesting.
  • Elevated cortisol: cortisol reaches its natural peak in the morning. Running fasted can amplify this response. In moderate doses, cortisol helps mobilize energy; in excess (long or intense sessions), it promotes muscle catabolism.
Key fact: Your body has about 2,000 kcal stored as glycogen, but over 50,000 kcal as fat (even in lean people). Teaching your metabolism to efficiently access those lipid reserves is the goal of fasted training.

Science-backed benefits

1. Greater fat oxidation during exercise

This is the most documented benefit. When you run fasted, your body oxidizes 20% to 30% more fat during the session compared to running after breakfast, according to studies published in journals like the British Journal of Nutrition. This does not mean you lose more weight (weight loss depends on total caloric balance), but your body becomes more efficient at using fat as fuel.

2. Metabolic flexibility

Metabolic flexibility is your body's ability to switch between carbohydrates and fats as an energy source based on demand. Runners with good metabolic flexibility better conserve their glycogen stores during long races, delaying the dreaded “bonk.” Occasionally training fasted is one of the most effective ways to develop this adaptation.

3. Mitochondrial biogenesis

Recent research suggests that training with low glycogen availability can stimulate mitochondrial biogenesis, meaning the creation of new mitochondria in muscle cells. More mitochondria means more “energy power plants” producing ATP, which translates to greater aerobic endurance in the medium and long term.

4. Simplicity and digestive comfort

This benefit is more practical than physiological, but no less real. Many runners suffer gastrointestinal issues (nausea, cramps, reflux) when running shortly after eating. Running fasted eliminates this problem entirely. You do not need to wake up earlier to digest breakfast or worry about which foods sit well before running. You wake up, hydrate and head out.

Important: These benefits are mainly obtained with low to moderate intensity exercise. At high intensity, your body needs glucose no matter what, and performance drops significantly without it.

Real risks you should know

Muscle catabolism in long sessions

When glycogen reserves are depleted and energy demand exceeds what fat oxidation can provide, your body begins breaking down muscle proteins to convert amino acids into glucose (gluconeogenesis). This is more likely in fasted sessions exceeding 60-75 minutes, especially at medium or high intensities. If your goal is to gain or maintain muscle mass, long fasted sessions work against you.

Performance drop in high intensity

High-intensity workouts (intervals, tempo, hills, repeats) fundamentally depend on glucose as fuel. Running without adequate glycogen stores reduces your ability to sustain fast paces, shortens time to exhaustion and compromises workout quality. If you are doing a quality session, eat before.

Hypoglycemia symptoms

Not all runners tolerate fasted exercise equally well. Some people experience dizziness, trembling, cold sweats, blurred vision, confusion or extreme weakness. These symptoms are signs that your blood sugar has dropped too low. If you experience them, stop immediately, consume something with fast-acting sugar (juice, gel, fruit) and do not try again without consulting a doctor.

Not for everyone

Fasted running is contraindicated or requires medical supervision in these cases:

  • Diabetics: both type 1 and type 2 on hypoglycemic medication. The risk of severe hypoglycemia is real and dangerous.
  • People with a history of eating disorders: fasting can reinforce food restriction patterns and food obsession.
  • Pregnant or breastfeeding women: additional nutritional demands make fasting inappropriate.
  • People with reactive hypoglycemia: those who experience sudden blood sugar drops easily.

Who should try it?

Fasted training can be a useful tool for certain runner profiles, always under specific conditions:

  • Experienced runners who already have a good aerobic base and know their limits well. This is not a technique for someone who just started running.
  • Only for easy and recovery runs: sessions at a comfortable pace where you can maintain a conversation without difficulty. Heart rate zone 2 at most.
  • Sessions under 60 minutes: that is the safe limit for most people. Well-adapted runners can go up to 75 minutes, but no more.
  • Ultra or marathon runners who want to improve their fat-burning efficiency as specific preparation for long races.
  • People who suffer digestive issues when running after eating and prefer to train on an empty stomach.
Golden rule: Train fasted to adapt your metabolism. Race fueled to maximize your performance. These are different objectives that require different strategies.

Who should avoid it?

  • Beginners: if you have been running regularly for less than 6 months, you do not yet have the aerobic base or experience to manage your body's signals while fasted. First build your base endurance and learn your limits.
  • High-intensity sessions: intervals, fartlek, tempo, hills. These workouts need available glycogen to be executed with quality. Doing them fasted will only make you perform worse and accumulate more fatigue.
  • Long runs over 90 minutes: long runs are the most demanding workout of the week. Doing them fasted multiplies the risk of catabolism, hypoglycemia and extreme fatigue. Eat before and during.
  • People with medical conditions: diabetes, eating disorders, reactive hypoglycemia, pregnancy or any condition affecting glucose metabolism.
  • Race days: never race fasted. On race day you need all your energy reserves at their peak.

How to start safely: step-by-step protocol

Weeks 1-2: Initial adaptation

Start with just 1 fasted session per week, on an easy run day. Limit duration to 20-30 minutes at a very comfortable pace. Your body needs to get used to mobilizing fats efficiently, and that does not happen overnight.

Weeks 3-4: Gradual progression

If you tolerate the first sessions well (no dizziness, no excessive fatigue, no sudden mood changes), gradually increase duration to 35-45 minutes. Keep the pace easy. You can add a second weekly session if you feel comfortable.

Week 5 onwards: Consolidation

With 4 weeks of adaptation, you can reach 45-60 minute fasted sessions, always at an easy pace. You do not need (and should not) do more than 2-3 fasted sessions per week. The rest of your workouts, especially quality sessions, should be done fueled.

Hydration: non-negotiable

Running fasted does not mean running dehydrated. Drink 300-500 ml of water in the 30 minutes before heading out. You have gone all night without drinking and your body is slightly dehydrated. Black coffee (no sugar or milk) is also compatible with fasting and can improve your performance. Check our hydration guide for runners for more details.

Warning signs: when to stop

Always carry a gel or bar just in case. If during your run you experience any of these symptoms, stop immediately and consume fast-acting carbohydrates:

  • Dizziness or feeling unsteady
  • Blurred vision or light spots
  • Trembling in hands or legs
  • Sudden cold sweats
  • Confusion or difficulty thinking clearly
  • Extreme weakness or legs that “won't respond”

What to eat after a fasted run

Post-workout nutrition is especially important when you have run fasted, because your body has gone many hours without nutrients and has just undergone physical effort. The 30-60 minute window afterward is the optimal time to replenish.

The three pillars of post-run breakfast

  • Protein (20-30 g): to stop catabolism and kickstart muscle repair. Eggs, Greek yogurt, protein powder, cottage cheese or tuna.
  • Carbohydrates (40-60 g): to replenish depleted glycogen. Oatmeal, whole wheat bread, fruit, rice or toast.
  • Healthy fats (small amount): avocado, nuts or olive oil. They provide satiety and essential nutrients, but do not overdo it so you do not slow down protein and carbohydrate absorption.

Practical post-fasted-run breakfast examples

  1. Quick option: banana + oatmeal + milk + protein powder + tablespoon of peanut butter smoothie.
  2. Classic option: 2 scrambled eggs + whole wheat toast with avocado + piece of fruit.
  3. Complete option: Greek yogurt + granola + blueberries + walnuts + honey.

The most important thing is not to delay this meal. The sooner you eat after a fasted session, the sooner you stop catabolism and begin recovery. For more ideas, visit our nutrition guide for runners.

Practical tip: Prepare your breakfast before heading out to run. That way when you return you only need to heat it up or assemble it. When you arrive tired and hungry, the last thing you want is to cook from scratch.

Frequently asked questions

Does fasted running burn more fat than running after breakfast?

Yes, during the session more fat is oxidized because glycogen and insulin levels are low. However, long-term studies show that total fat loss depends on daily caloric balance, not when you run. Fasted running improves your ability to use fat as fuel, but it does not cause more weight loss by itself if total calories are equal.

How long can I safely run on an empty stomach?

For most runners, the safe limit is between 45 and 60 minutes at an easy pace. Experienced and well-adapted runners can go up to 75 minutes, but beyond that duration the risk of hypoglycemia, muscle catabolism and performance decline increases considerably. Never do long runs or high-intensity sessions fasted.

Can I have coffee before a fasted run?

Yes. Black coffee (no sugar or milk) does not break the metabolic fast. Additionally, caffeine improves fatty acid mobilization, delays perceived fatigue and can enhance performance. Have 1-2 cups about 20-30 minutes before heading out. Make sure to also drink water to counteract the diuretic effect.

Does fasted running cause muscle loss?

In short sessions (under 60 minutes) and at low intensity, muscle loss is minimal. The risk of catabolism increases in long or intense sessions, when the body turns to muscle amino acids as an energy source. To minimize it, limit duration, keep intensity low and consume quality protein within 30 minutes after your workout.

Is it safe to run fasted if I am diabetic?

It is not recommended without medical supervision. People with type 1 or type 2 diabetes who take hypoglycemic medication are at risk of severe hypoglycemia when running fasted. If you have diabetes, consult your endocrinologist before trying it and never do it without prior blood glucose monitoring.
