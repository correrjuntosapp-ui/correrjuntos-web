Beetroot is one of the few sports supplements with genuine scientific backing, and also one of the most misunderstood. The interest is not in the vitamins or the colour, but in the nitrate: your body turns it into nitric oxide, a molecule involved in blood flow and in how efficiently your muscles use oxygen. In running, cycling, triathlon and any endurance sport, that translates into a small but measurable edge in some efforts.

The catch is that not every "beetroot" product delivers the same amount of nitrate, and many do not even state it. What beetroot nitrates are

Nitrate (NO₃⁻) is a compound found naturally in leafy green vegetables and in beetroot. When you ingest it, part of it concentrates in your saliva and the bacteria in your mouth reduce it to nitrite. This step is essential, because your own cells cannot do it. Once you swallow, the nitrite reaches tissues with low oxygen and low pH — like a muscle working hard — and there it is finally converted into nitric oxide, which regulates blood flow and muscle efficiency. This is the nitrate → nitrite → nitric oxide pathway.

The evidence suggests this pathway can slightly reduce the oxygen cost of exercise (that is, improve "economy"), but let's be honest: the effect is small, variable and not guaranteed. According to recent reviews and the Australian Institute of Sport (AIS), which classifies dietary nitrate in its highest-evidence tier (Group A), it tends to help recreational or mid-level runners more than highly trained athletes, and more in intense efforts of 2 to 10 minutes than in very long events.

There is an important practical difference between eating beetroot and taking a concentrated supplement. The nitrate content of a beetroot varies a lot with soil, climate and harvest, so the vegetable gives you an unpredictable amount. A standardized concentrate or extract that declares the milligrams per dose lets you get close to a reproducible dose. Whole beetroot is still an excellent food; it is simply not the most reliable tool when you want a specific dose before competing.

In one line: nitrates can contribute to a small efficiency gain in certain efforts, but the actual amount of nitrate matters a lot and not every athlete responds the same way. Pair supplementation with a structured training plan if you really want to perform.

And a vocabulary warning that clears up almost every misunderstanding on this topic. These are not the same:

  • Milligrams of nitrate (what actually matters for the ergogenic effect).
  • Milligrams of beetroot extract (not the same as nitrate: a 3,000 mg extract does not provide 3,000 mg of nitrate).
  • Grams of powder per dose (the total amount of product).
  • Milligrams of beetroot equivalent in 10:1 or 25:1 concentrates (a marketing figure that says nothing about nitrate).

So when you see "declared nitrate" in the table, we mean only the milligrams of nitrate the manufacturer explicitly states; where it is not declared, we say so clearly instead of inventing a number. How to choose a beetroot nitrate supplement

For a quick decision, use these rules:

  • If you want a reliable dose before competing: choose a product that declares the milligrams of nitrate per serving (a shot or powder), in the approximate range of 5-9 mmol (about 310-560 mg). Beet It, Amix, 226ERS and the Scientiffic Nutrition shot declare it.
  • If you prioritise convenience on race day: a ready-to-drink shot avoids scales and scoops.
  • If you train or race over long distances: a drink that combines nitrate with carbohydrates and electrolytes covers fuel and hydration at once.
  • If you prefer beetroot as a food: a juice or 100% beetroot powder is a natural route, though it does not guarantee an exact dose.
  • If you value convenience over dose: capsules are practical, but rarely state the real nitrate content.
  • If you compete under anti-doping control: prioritise a product with third-party certification such as Informed Sport.

Two warnings that apply to any choice: avoid antibacterial mouthwash around the time of your dose, because it blocks the nitrate conversion, and test it in training first, never on race day. If you take medication or have a health condition, consult a healthcare professional first.

How much nitrate you need (mmol and mg)

Here is the key that almost no label explains. The dose is measured in two ways: in millimoles (mmol), which count the number of molecules, and in milligrams (mg), which measure mass. The conversion is simple: 1 mmol of nitrate equals about 62 mg.

The acute dose the literature associates with an ergogenic effect is usually around 5-9 mmol of nitrate, i.e. roughly 310-560 mg. The Australian Institute of Sport indicates a minimum of about 5-6 mmol per dose and adds that exceeding 10-12 mmol brings no further benefit. In practice: a 400 mg shot or a 500 mg dose are within the reasonable range; a single beetroot capsule almost never is.

Two honest warnings. First: do not assume every beetroot powder provides an effective amount of nitrate; many do not even declare it and their content varies by batch. Second: there is no single universal dose valid for everyone, because the response depends on your oral microbiome, your diet and your training level. Start from the range a reliable product declares and adjust from there.

When to take them

There are two main strategies, both supported by the literature:

  • Acute (one-off) dose: blood nitrite — the bioactive form — tends to peak around 2-3 hours after intake. That is why many protocols recommend taking nitrate 2 to 3 hours before the race or key session.
  • Multi-day load: taking a daily dose for roughly 3 to 7 days before the event. This is the strategy suggested, for example, by brands like 226ERS for high-load or competition weeks.

In training, the main use is twofold: to test your tolerance and to see whether you respond, because not everyone does. In competition, apply what you have already rehearsed; never try a new supplement on race day. And a golden rule of sports nutrition: what works is consistency with your training plan and your nutrition strategy, not a single miracle product.

The mouthwash detail

This point surprises a lot of people and is well documented. Because the nitrate-to-nitrite step depends entirely on the bacteria in your mouth, anything that removes them blocks the pathway. Antibacterial mouthwashes (for example, chlorhexidine ones) drastically reduce that oral conversion: in controlled studies, rinsing with an antibacterial mouthwash markedly blunts the rise in blood nitrite after a nitrate dose.

The practical takeaway is direct: if you are going to use beetroot for a goal, avoid antibacterial mouthwash around the time of your dose. The Australian Institute of Sport mentions this explicitly among its recommendations. It is not a minor curiosity: it is one reason why beetroot "does nothing" for some people.

Safety and precautions

Nitrate from beetroot and vegetables is generally considered safe for healthy people, and it should not be confused with the nitrites added to processed meats: they do not share the same risk profile, partly because beetroot also provides antioxidants that limit the formation of problematic compounds. The EFSA sets the acceptable daily intake of nitrate at around 3.7 mg per kilo of body weight per day. That said, there are points to keep in mind:

  • Gastrointestinal discomfort: possible, especially with juices and concentrates. Test in training first.
  • Reddish urine or stool (beeturia): harmless and temporary. It is not blood; it is the beetroot pigment.
  • Blood pressure: nitrate tends to lower it. This matters if you already take antihypertensive medication or other vasodilators.
  • Medication: be careful with vasodilators (for example, PDE5 inhibitors) and blood thinners; check first if you take chronic medication.
  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding: several manufacturers advise against it; consult your healthcare professional.
  • Kidney or cardiovascular disease: always assess supplementation with a professional.
  • Combining with other supplements: it is compatible with gels and carbohydrate drinks; if a product contained caffeine (not the case here), factor that in.
  • Avoid buying pure nitrite salts: they are a different thing and carry risks; beetroot and these supplements are not that.
ⓘ If in any doubt — and especially if you have a health condition, are pregnant or breastfeeding, or take medication — consult a healthcare professional before supplementing. This article does not replace that advice.

Frequently asked questions

Does beetroot really improve performance?

The evidence suggests dietary nitrate from beetroot can slightly reduce the oxygen cost of exercise and help in intense efforts of 2 to 10 minutes, but the effect is small, variable and not guaranteed. It tends to help recreational or mid-level runners more than highly trained athletes. It does not replace training, rest or nutrition.

When should you take nitrates before running?

Blood nitrite tends to peak around 2-3 hours after intake, so many protocols recommend taking them 2 to 3 hours before. There is also a multi-day load, with a daily dose for roughly 3 to 7 days before the event. This is general information, not an individual prescription.

How much nitrate should a good supplement provide?

The acute dose associated with an effect is usually around 5-9 mmol of nitrate, roughly 310-560 mg (1 mmol equals about 62 mg). The Australian Institute of Sport indicates a minimum of about 5-6 mmol per dose and that exceeding 10-12 mmol adds no more benefit. That is why it helps to choose products that declare the milligrams of nitrate per dose.

Is powder, capsules or a shot better?

It depends on your priority. Shots and powders that declare the milligrams of nitrate make a reproducible dose easier. Capsules are convenient and portable, but many do not state the real nitrate content, so it is hard to know whether they deliver an effective dose. Whole beetroot powder is a good food, but its nitrate content varies a lot by batch.

Can you take them daily?

Many loading protocols use a daily dose for several consecutive days before a competition. Long-term chronic use has not been studied exhaustively, so it is reasonable not to make it an indefinite habit without a clear reason. If in doubt, consult a healthcare professional.

Are they useful for 5K, half marathon and marathon?

The evidence is more consistent for intense efforts of a few minutes and for intermittent exercise than for very long events. It may make sense for a 5K and demanding sections of a half marathon; for a marathon the effect is less clear. Because it varies a lot between individuals, test it in training first.

Does beetroot contain caffeine?

No. Beetroot and beetroot nitrate supplements do not naturally contain caffeine, and the products in this comparison are, by composition, caffeine-free. If you want a caffeine effect, you would need to add it separately and with caution.

Can I combine them with gels and carbohydrate drinks?

Yes. Beetroot nitrate is compatible with energy gels and hydration drinks, which cover the need for fuel during the effort. Remember to avoid antibacterial mouthwash around the time of your dose, because it kills the mouth bacteria that convert nitrate into nitrite.

Why do many products not state the nitrate content?

Because the nitrate content of beetroot varies a lot with soil, climate and harvest, and few products are independently tested. That is why many beetroot powders and capsules do not declare the milligrams per dose. Standardized extracts and concentrates that do declare it offer a more reproducible dose.

It is a more complete formula than a simple beetroot powder. Amix provided it for us to test; the brand did not determine its position or our assessment.
