Why hydration matters for runners

Three key factors determine how well a hydration drink works for running:

  • Electrolytes: Sodium is the most critical electrolyte lost through sweat. A good running drink should contain 300-700mg of sodium per liter. Potassium, magnesium and calcium also play important roles in muscle function and preventing cramps.
  • Carbohydrates: For runs over 90 minutes, carbs in your drink provide fuel alongside hydration. The optimal concentration is 6-8% (60-80g per liter) for isotonic drinks. Too much causes stomach problems; too little and you miss the fueling benefit.
  • Osmolality: This measures how concentrated the drink is compared to your blood. Isotonic (same as blood) and hypotonic (lower than blood) drinks are absorbed fastest. Hypertonic drinks sit in the stomach longer and can cause GI distress during high-intensity running.
Tip: Never try a new hydration product on race day. Test everything in training first. Your stomach needs 3-4 sessions to adapt to a new formula. What works for your training partner might cause GI distress for you — hydration is deeply personal.

Frequently asked questions

How much water should I drink while running?

A general guideline is 150-250ml every 15-20 minutes during runs longer than 60 minutes. However, sweat rates vary hugely between individuals. A better approach is to weigh yourself before and after a long run: every kilogram lost equals roughly 1 liter of fluid deficit. Aim to replace 80% of sweat losses during the run and the rest afterward.

Do I need electrolytes or is plain water enough?

For runs under 60 minutes, plain water is usually sufficient. For longer runs, especially in hot weather, electrolyte drinks are recommended because you lose sodium, potassium and magnesium through sweat. Sodium is the most important electrolyte to replace: losing too much without replacing it can lead to hyponatremia, a dangerous condition where blood sodium levels drop too low.

What is the difference between isotonic, hypotonic and hypertonic drinks?

Isotonic drinks have a similar concentration to blood and are absorbed quickly while providing carbs and electrolytes — most sports drinks fall in this category. Hypotonic drinks have a lower concentration and are absorbed fastest, ideal for pure hydration without much energy. Hypertonic drinks have higher concentration and provide more energy but are absorbed more slowly and can cause stomach issues during running.

Can I make my own electrolyte drink at home?

Yes. A simple recipe is: 500ml water, a pinch of salt (about 1/4 teaspoon for 300mg sodium), 2 tablespoons of honey or maple syrup, and a squeeze of lemon. This provides basic electrolytes and carbohydrates. However, commercial options offer more precise and consistent formulations, which matters for race day when you need reliability (Runner's World Nutrition).

Should I use the same hydration drink for training and racing?

Yes, always test your race nutrition in training first. Your stomach needs to adapt to any hydration product, and race day is not the time to experiment. Use your chosen drink in at least 3-4 long training runs before racing with it. Also check what the race provides at aid stations and practice with that brand if you plan to use on-course nutrition rather than carrying your own.
