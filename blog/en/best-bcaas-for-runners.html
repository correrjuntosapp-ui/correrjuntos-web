8 Best BCAAs for Runners 2026

BCAAs (branched-chain amino acids) are one of the most widely used supplements in endurance running, and for good reason. Leucine, isoleucine and valine represent around 35% of the essential amino acids present in skeletal muscle, and are significantly depleted during long training sessions. The International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) has recognized that BCAA supplementation can reduce exercise-induced muscle damage and decrease perceived fatigue during prolonged efforts (World Athletics) (ACSM).

When you run for more than 60-90 minutes, your body starts using BCAAs as an energy source, which accelerates their muscular depletion. Additionally, BCAAs compete with tryptophan to cross the blood-brain barrier: by maintaining high BCAA levels, you reduce tryptophan entry to the brain and, with it, the serotonin production that causes central fatigue. In 2026, the market offers dozens of formulas, but not all offer the right ratio, purity or format for runners. We have selected the 8 best BCAAs so you can find the ideal one for your training type.

What are BCAAs and why they matter for runners

BCAA stands for Branched-Chain Amino Acids. They are three essential amino acids that your body cannot produce on its own and must be obtained from diet or supplementation:

  • Leucine: The most important amino acid for activating muscle protein synthesis through the mTOR pathway. It is the main driver of muscle tissue repair and building after running. This is why BCAA ratios always prioritize leucine (2:1:1, 4:1:1, etc.).
  • Isoleucine: Involved in glucose regulation and muscular energy production. It helps muscles uptake glucose during exercise, which is especially useful on long runs where glycogen stores are progressively depleted.
  • Valine: Competes directly with tryptophan for blood-brain barrier transporters. By maintaining high valine levels in the blood, you block excessive tryptophan entry to the brain, delaying the onset of central fatigue during long-distance races.
Tip: For most runners, a BCAA with a 2:1:1 ratio is the most balanced and scientifically supported choice. Powder format allows you to drink it during long runs, while capsules are more convenient to take before or after. BCAAs vs EAAs vs Protein: what do you really need

It is one of the most common questions among runners who want to optimize their supplementation. These are the key differences:

  • BCAAs (3 amino acids): Leucine, isoleucine and valine. Rapidly absorbed, light on the stomach and can be consumed during exercise. Ideal for reducing fatigue and protecting muscle during long runs. Not sufficient on their own for complete recovery.
  • EAAs (9 essential amino acids): Include the 3 BCAAs plus 6 other essential amino acids. They offer a more complete profile for protein synthesis. A superior alternative to BCAAs alone if you can only choose one product, though slightly more expensive.
  • Whey protein (all amino acids): Contains all 20 amino acids, including the 9 essentials. The most complete supplement for post-workout recovery. Heavier on the stomach, not recommended during exercise.
Editor's recommendation: The ideal combination for serious runners is: BCAAs before and during training (muscle protection + anti-fatigue) and whey protein after training (complete recovery). If your budget is limited and you already take whey after training, BCAAs add value when you train fasted or in sessions over 90 minutes.

BCAA dosage for runners

The optimal BCAA dose depends on your body weight, the duration of your workouts and the timing of intake. These are the evidence-based recommendations:

  • Recreational runners (3-4 days/week): 5g of BCAAs before or after training. A single serving is enough to protect muscle and reduce soreness in sessions up to 60 minutes.
  • Intermediate runners (5-6 days/week): 5-10g per day, split between before and during training. On long run days (+90 min), carry BCAAs in your bottle to sip during the session.
  • Marathoners and ultra trail runners: 10-15g on long session days. Take 5g before, 5g during and 5g after. On ultra trails, you can maintain an intake of 5g every 2-3 hours of effort.
  • Fasted training: 5-10g of BCAAs 15-20 minutes before heading out to run. This is the scenario where BCAAs have the greatest impact, as they protect muscle from breakdown when no dietary amino acids are available.

In powder format, mix BCAAs with 400-500ml of cold water and sip during training. In capsule format, take them 30 minutes before training with a glass of water. BCAAs are absorbed in 15-30 minutes, much faster than complete protein, making them ideal for moments when you need amino acids immediately.

Important: BCAAs are a complement, not a substitute for a protein-rich diet. If you already consume 1.4-1.8g of protein per kg of body weight per day from complete sources (meat, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes), BCAAs add value primarily during and before training, especially fasted or in long sessions. Do not expect miracles if your nutritional base is deficient.

Frequently asked questions

What are BCAAs and what are they used for in running?

BCAAs (branched-chain amino acids) are three essential amino acids: leucine, isoleucine and valine. They represent approximately 35% of the amino acids present in skeletal muscle. For runners, BCAAs help reduce muscle damage during long workouts, decrease central fatigue by blocking tryptophan entry to the brain, speed up post-workout recovery and can preserve muscle mass during high-mileage periods.

What is the best BCAA ratio for runners?

The most studied and recommended ratio for runners is 2:1:1 (leucine:isoleucine:valine). This ratio replicates the natural proportion of these amino acids in muscle and has the strongest scientific backing. Higher ratios like 4:1:1 or 8:1:1 prioritize leucine to stimulate protein synthesis, but may unbalance isoleucine and valine absorption. For most runners, 2:1:1 is the safest and most effective choice.

When should runners take BCAAs?

The optimal time depends on your goal. Before training (30 minutes before): reduces fatigue during long sessions and protects muscle if training fasted. During training: ideal for runs over 90 minutes, mixed with water. After training: speeds muscle recovery, especially if you cannot eat a full meal within 30-60 minutes. The recommended dose is 5-10g per serving.

BCAAs or whey protein, which is better for runners?

It depends on the timing and situation. Whey protein contains the three BCAAs plus the other 6 essential amino acids, making it more complete for post-workout recovery. However, BCAAs in powder form are absorbed faster, are lighter on the stomach during exercise and can be consumed during long runs without causing gastric discomfort. The ideal combination is: BCAAs during or before training, and whey protein after. If you can only choose one, whey protein is more complete.

Do BCAAs have side effects for runners?

BCAAs are safe at recommended doses (5-20g daily) for most healthy people. Side effects are rare and include mild gastrointestinal discomfort if taken on an empty stomach at high doses, and possible interference with diabetes medications. They are not recommended for people with ALS (amyotrophic lateral sclerosis) or maple syrup urine disease. For healthy runners, BCAAs are one of the supplements with the best safety profile on the market.
